In every major construction or renovation project, gypsum works don't happen in isolation  -  they coordinate closely with electrical installations, plumbing, HVAC systems and other trades. This coordination is the key to a successful project, and our experience on projects like Vlora International Airport has taught us exactly how to manage this process.

In this article, we share our experience coordinating [professional gypsum works](/en/gypsum-works/) with other construction trades.

## Why Is Coordination Important?

A common mistake in construction projects is treating each trade as independent. In reality, gypsum works, electrical and plumbing are interconnected:

- **Electrical cables** run inside gypsum walls and ceilings
- **Plumbing pipes** must be installed before walls are closed
- **HVAC systems** integrate into suspended gypsum ceilings
- **Fire alarm and sprinkler systems** mount inside gypsum structures
- **LED spotlights and lighting** require precise openings in gypsum panels

Without proper coordination, you risk delays, extra costs and poor quality.

## The Phases of Coordination

### Phase 1: Planning and Design

Before any physical work begins, all teams must sit together. At Torra Gips, we actively participate in the planning phase to ensure the gypsum structure accommodates all installations. This saves time and money in later phases.

### Phase 2: Metal Framework (Skeleton)

The first physical step is mounting the metal framework that will support gypsum panels. During this phase, we leave openings for electrical boxes, create passages for pipes, and plan points for spotlights, cameras and sensors.

### Phase 3: Installations Inside the Structure

Once the metal framework is ready, installation teams begin their work  -  electrical cabling, plumbing, and HVAC ductwork all happen in this phase.

### Phase 4: Closing with Gypsum Panels

Only after all installations are completed and verified do we begin closing with gypsum panels. Every installation is documented with photos before closure, and access panels are preserved for future maintenance.

### Phase 5: Finishing

After closure, finishing works begin  -  [plastering](/en/wall-plastering/) of joints and surfaces, precise cutting for spotlights and switches, and [final painting](/en/painting/).

## The Most Common Coordination Mistakes

1. **Lack of pre-planning**  -  starting work without a detailed plan
2. **Not respecting work order**  -  each trade has its sequence
3. **Poor communication** between teams
4. **Cheap hidden materials**  -  low-quality electrical or plumbing inside walls is a recipe for expensive problems later
